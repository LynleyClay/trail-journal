When my friend asked me to do the Appalachian Trail with her, it felt a little ridiculous. I'd never even heard of it, and my family was definitely not the backpacking type. But I liked hiking, and I'd spent a lot of time doing it around my small eastern Kentucky university. I was about to graduate with nothing else lined up, so I figured, why not? That was basically the entire decision-making process. I tried to get in shape beforehand, but mostly I just copied my friend's gear list and called it preparation.

The night before I started, I was sitting on the back porch of a cabin in North Georgia, and it was way colder than I'd expected. The idea of sleeping in 30° weather for the foreseeable future made my stomach drop. But I was too far in to back out now, and way too embarrassed to admit I wanted to. My parents dropped me off at the Approach Trail, the unofficial start of the AT, at Amicalola Falls, and my dad tried one last time to hand me his miniature handgun. "I'll be fine," I said. "Okay," he replied, "give me a call in two weeks when you want to quit." At this point my dad knows exactly how to get me going. If there's one thing I can't stand, it's someone telling me I can't do something. Fine. I'm walking 2,000 miles. I don't know how, but I will.
